The problem we solve

Why does DaVinci Resolve have no audio from your videos?

If you've opened a clip in DaVinci Resolve and it shows no sound — or the audio track is greyed out — it's almost always an audio codec error, not a problem with your microphone or project.

Many cameras, recorders, and capture apps like OBS Studio, smartphones, or action cameras save audio with compressed codecs like AAC or MP3 inside MP4 or MOV containers. The free version of DaVinci Resolve — and especially DaVinci Resolve on Linux and in professional environments — does not include the licenses to decode all those audio formats. The result is the classic DaVinci Resolve no sound issue, desynced audio, or clips that won't even import.

The fix: change the container, not re-encode the video

VidToFLAC performs a remux: it repackages your file into an MKV container and converts only the audio track to FLAC, a lossless format that professional editing software decodes natively. When you convert video audio to FLAC this way, the video stream is copied bit by bit, without re-rendering a single frame.

This means three things for your workflow:

  • Zero quality loss: it's a lossless video remux, the original frames remain intact.
  • Speed: no video re-encoding, so the process takes seconds instead of minutes.
  • Immediate compatibility: the resulting MKV imports with audio in DaVinci Resolve, Premiere Pro and Avid without extra plugins or licenses.

And all of this happens with FFmpeg in the browser via WebAssembly: the same industry-standard tool professionals use, running locally on your machine.

Frequently asked questions

Common questions about FLAC conversion

Does VidToFLAC reduce my video quality?

No. The video stream is copied directly bit by bit, without reopening or recompressing frames: it's a remux, not a re-encode. Only the audio is converted to FLAC, a lossless format. The image you get is identical to the original file.

Why is FLAC better than extracting audio to a separate WAV?

Because keeping audio and video synchronized inside the same MKV file means you never have to manually align them in your editor's timeline. If you extract audio to a separate WAV you have to re-sync it clip by clip by hand; with FLAC inside the container you drag one file and everything lines up. FLAC also takes up less space than WAV at the same lossless quality.

Are my video files uploaded to any server?

Never. The entire conversion runs on your own device thanks to FFmpeg compiled to WebAssembly, running inside your browser. Your videos are not uploaded, do not pass through any server, and are not stored anywhere. It's 100% private and local: that's also why you can process multi-gigabyte files without any upload wait.

What input formats are supported?

Video: MP4, MOV, MKV, AVI, WebM, FLV, WMV, M4V, TS, M2TS, MPEG, 3GP, VOB and more. Audio: WAV, MP3, AAC, OGG, M4A, OPUS, WMA, AIFF and FLAC. If your clip uses a video codec the browser cannot play (DNxHD, ProRes, MJPEG…), VidToFLAC automatically re-encodes it to H.264 to ensure correct playback.

Does it work for DaVinci Resolve on Linux?

Yes. DaVinci Resolve on Linux is exactly where the audio codec error appears most, because it does not include AAC or MP3 decoders. By converting the audio to FLAC inside an MKV you get a file that DaVinci Resolve plays natively, restoring sound without installing paid codecs.

Is it free and is there a file size limit?

It's completely free and no sign-up required. Since processing happens on your own computer with FFmpeg (WebAssembly) and there are no file uploads, there is no server-imposed size limit: you can convert videos of several gigabytes; the only practical limit is your device's memory.

How to extract audio from a video to FLAC without losing quality?

VidToFLAC uses FFmpeg to perform a remux: it copies the video stream bit by bit without recompressing it, and re-encodes only the audio track to the official FLAC codec, which is lossless. The result is an MKV file with FLAC audio of quality identical to the original. Everything happens inside your browser via WebAssembly — without uploading the file to any server.

Is vidtoflac.tech safe to use?

Yes. VidToFLAC processes your files 100% locally thanks to FFmpeg compiled to WebAssembly: the video never leaves your device, it's not uploaded to any server, and it's not stored anywhere. Once you close the tab, the temporary files from the WASM virtual filesystem are deleted automatically. No user registration, no tracking cookies, no third party with access to your files.

What is the difference between MP3 and FLAC?

MP3 is a lossy format: it compresses audio by discarding frequencies the ear perceives least, reducing size but irreversibly degrading quality. FLAC is a lossless format: it compresses data without discarding any information, so quality is identical to the original. For professional video editing and high-fidelity archiving, FLAC is the recommended choice — and DaVinci Resolve supports it natively.
